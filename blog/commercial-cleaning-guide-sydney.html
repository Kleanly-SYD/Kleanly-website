How Often Should an Office Be Cleaned? A Commercial Cleaning Guide

10 April 2026
7 min read
By Kleanly

Office cleaning frequency is one of those decisions that most business owners make by feel — they either over-invest in daily cleaning that isn't necessary, or under-invest until something forces their hand (usually illness in the team or a client visit going badly).

The right cleaning schedule depends on a handful of factors: how many people use the space, what they do there, what type of surfaces are present, and what industry you're in. This guide breaks it down clearly so you can make an evidence-based decision for your Sydney workplace.

The Short Answer: A Baseline by Headcount

If you're looking for a starting point before reading further, here's a practical guide by team size:

Team SizeRecommended FrequencyNotes
1–5 peopleWeeklyLow traffic; fortnightly may be sufficient
6–20 people2–3× per weekKitchen and bathrooms need more attention
21–50 peopleDaily or 5× per weekHigh-touch surfaces accumulate bacteria quickly
50+ peopleDaily + periodic deep cleansShould include waste, common areas, and sanitising
Any size (retail/hospitality)Daily minimumCustomer-facing — standards are non-negotiable

What Drives the Frequency?

Headcount is only part of the equation. These factors can push your frequency up significantly:

  • Client-facing environment: If clients visit the premises, the bar is higher regardless of team size. First impressions form in seconds.
  • Food handling or preparation: Any kitchen, cafe, or food prep area requires daily cleaning — and in many commercial settings, it's a legal requirement.
  • High-touch surfaces: Reception desks, lift buttons, shared equipment, kitchen bench tops, door handles. These need wiping down daily in a team of any meaningful size.
  • Carpeted floors: Carpet traps allergens and requires more frequent vacuuming than hard floors. In a high-traffic area, daily vacuuming prevents compacted dirt and extends carpet life.
  • Sensitive industry: Healthcare, legal, accounting, and childcare environments often have hygiene obligations beyond standard office settings.

The Case for Periodic Deep Cleans

Regular maintenance cleaning handles daily dirt, but it doesn't replace a deep clean. Quarterly deep cleans address the accumulation that routine cleaning misses:

  • Behind and underneath furniture and equipment
  • Air conditioning filters and vents
  • Blind and curtain cleaning
  • Upholstered furniture (chairs, lounges)
  • High surfaces, light fittings, and skirting boards
  • Commercial carpet steam cleaning

Think of deep cleans as a reset: they bring the baseline hygiene level back to zero and extend the life of surfaces and furnishings.

What Happens When You Under-Clean

Most business owners accept a degree of "managed messiness" without realising what it costs. Studies consistently show that dirty workplaces correlate with:

  • Higher absenteeism — viruses and bacteria spread through shared surfaces at a measurable rate
  • Lower productivity — cluttered, dirty environments reduce focus and increase cognitive load
  • Negative client perception — in surveys, a dirty office ranks as one of the top reasons potential clients don't proceed
  • Faster degradation of surfaces — grease on kitchen benches, ground-in carpet dirt, limescale on tapware all get harder to remove the longer they're left
For Sydney businesses: Sydney's humidity — particularly in summer — accelerates mould growth in poorly ventilated office kitchens and bathrooms. Increasing bathroom cleaning frequency in the December–March period is worth the small additional cost.

Building a Cleaning Schedule That Actually Works

The most effective commercial cleaning arrangements share a few traits:

  1. Written scope of works — a clear list of what is and isn't included, not a vague "general clean." This protects both you and the cleaning company.
  2. Consistent timing — out of hours cleaning (early morning or evening) means no disruption to the team and allows full access to all areas.
  3. Accountability structure — a check-in process so issues are identified and resolved quickly rather than left until the next scheduled clean.
  4. Flexibility for events — the ability to add a clean before a major client visit or after a team event without navigating a complex contract.
